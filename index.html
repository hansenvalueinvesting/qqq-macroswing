How the model works ▸

The idea. A Hidden Markov Model (HMM) assumes the market is always in one of a few invisible "regimes" (here: LONG, SHORT, CHOP), and each regime produces returns with a characteristic mean and volatility. We can't observe the regime directly — only the returns — so we infer it.

The math, in three pieces.

  1. Initial probabilities π — chance the chain starts in each state.
  2. Transition matrix A — chance of moving from state i to state j between bars.
  3. Emission distributions — each state has a full-covariance bivariate Gaussian over (20-day mean log return, 10-day realized volatility). Full covariance is important: bear days are jointly low-return and high-vol, and that cross-feature correlation is exactly what separates a real bear cluster from "low-return-but-calm" chop. A diagonal model throws this signal away.

The features. The return feature is a 20-day mean of daily log returns — a single-day return on QQQ is too noisy for the per-state Gaussians to distinguish (sign flips constantly), so the model needs a smoothed directional signal to anchor LONG vs. SHORT. The vol feature is a standard 10-day realized volatility (sample std of the prior 10 log returns) — direction-agnostic stress proxy.

Why 10 years of training data. EM needs a strong signal to find well-separated state Gaussians. A 10-year window for QQQ contains Q4 2018, March 2020, all of 2022, and other clear stress periods — these anchor the "SHORT" state cluster firmly and let the other two regimes sort themselves around it. On a 2-year window of mostly trending data, the model has to invent a bear state from scraps, and the boundaries blur.

Stickiness prior. The M-step adds Dirichlet pseudocounts to the transition matrix (50 on the diagonal, 1 off-diagonal) — a soft prior that says "regimes are persistent unless the data strongly disagrees." Without it, EM is happy to learn a near-uniform transition matrix that flips on residual noise.

Post-hoc smoothing. After Viterbi decoding, runs shorter than 3 bars are merged into the longer neighboring run. This removes single-bar flickers — a one-day blip is not a regime change.

Training. We run Baum-Welch, an Expectation-Maximization algorithm: (E-step) given current parameters, compute the probability of being in each state on each day via the forward-backward algorithm; (M-step) re-estimate π, A, and the Gaussians using those probabilities as soft assignments. Repeat until log-likelihood stops improving meaningfully. We do this from several random initializations and keep the best fit.

Decoding. The Viterbi algorithm finds the single most likely sequence of regimes (the colored bands on the chart). The probability ribbon shows the softer per-day posteriors.

Labeling. The three trained states are anonymous numbers; we relabel by mean return — highest = LONG, lowest = SHORT, middle = CHOP — so the colors stay consistent across retrainings.

What it is not. The regime is descriptive — it explains what likely happened, not what will happen next. The most recent label can revise as new bars arrive (the model re-smooths the whole history). This is not investment advice.
