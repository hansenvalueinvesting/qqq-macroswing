How the model works ▸

The idea. A Hidden Markov Model (HMM) assumes the market is always in one of a few invisible "regimes" (here: just Bull / Bear, or LONG / SHORT), and each regime produces daily returns with a characteristic mean and variance. We can't observe the regime directly — only the returns — so we infer it.

The math, in three pieces.

  1. Initial probabilities π — chance the chain starts in each state.
  2. Transition matrix A — 2×2 here, chance of moving from state i to state j between bars.
  3. Emission distributions — each state has a univariate Gaussian over the 1-day log return. Two parameters per state (mean μ, variance σ²) — no other features.

The feature. Single observation per day: r_t = ln(Close_t / Close_{t-1}). No vol feature, no trend-MSE, no smoothing — just raw daily log return. Yuan/Mitra (2019) show on FTSE 100 and Euro Stoxx 50 data that adding more features doesn't beat this on BIC; the dominant signal separating bull from bear is the variance of returns, not their mean (bear regimes have ~70× the daily-return variance of bull regimes in their fit).

Why 2 states and not 3+. Yuan/Mitra formally test K=2, 3, 4 with AIC and BIC. AIC picks K=4 (it always rewards complexity less heavily), but BIC — which penalizes complexity more strictly — picks K=2. They go with K=2 because (a) BIC is the stricter test, (b) parameter-estimation error compounds with more states, and (c) the third state in K=3 doesn't carve a clean cluster (their fit comment: "the other two states fit well except state 3"). Our previous K=3 setups echoed this — the CHOP state kept absorbing whichever days didn't fit cleanly. Going to K=2 eliminates that whole problem.

Training. Baum-Welch, an Expectation-Maximization algorithm: (E-step) given current parameters, compute the probability of being in each state on each day via the forward-backward algorithm; (M-step) re-estimate π, A, μ, σ² using those probabilities as soft assignments. Repeat until log-likelihood stops improving. We do several random initializations and keep the best fit.

Decoding. The Viterbi algorithm finds the single most likely sequence of regimes (the colored bands on the chart). The probability ribbon below the chart shows the softer per-day posteriors from forward-backward. The backtest trades the Viterbi labels directly.

Labeling. The two trained states are anonymous numbers; we relabel by mean return — higher mean → LONG (Bull), lower mean → SHORT (Bear) — so colors stay consistent across retrainings.

Training caveat. Many HMM regime papers retrain the model daily on a rolling window. This is computationally impractical in-browser — we train once on the user-selected training range and apply the trained model to the display window. Expect some parameter look-ahead unless the display window is fully out-of-sample relative to the training range.

What it is not. The regime is descriptive — it explains what likely happened, not what will happen next. The most recent label can revise as new bars arrive (the model re-smooths the whole history). This is not investment advice.
